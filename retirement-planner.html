Return outlook

How optimistic should the simulation be about future returns? The Monte Carlo still explores 10,000 random paths - this picks the mean of the distribution. Three numbers below are stock / bond / cash real return assumptions.

Taxes

Replaces the basic "effective tax rate" with 2026 federal brackets plus standard deduction. Ordinary income brackets only (no separate LTCG / qualified-dividends rates yet).

Portfolio fees

Subtracted from each year's return. A typical index fund is 0.03-0.15%; a managed fund is 0.5-1.0%; an advisor adds another 0.5-1.5%.

Social Security claim strategy

Your benefit at full retirement age (67). Claiming earlier reduces it, waiting until 70 increases it. Leave 0 to use the basic "Social Security (annual)" input as-is.

Required Minimum Distributions

Starting at age 73, the IRS requires you to withdraw a fraction of your tax-deferred (401k/IRA) balance each year whether you need the money or not. Advanced mode applies this automatically using the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table.

Career stages

One row per phase of your career. Salary is flat within a stage. For a raise or role change, add another row at that age. Rows are auto-sorted by age when you run the simulation.

Assumptions and limitations

Always on

  • Real (today's) dollars throughout. Inflation is not added back.
  • 10,000 Monte Carlo paths. Each path samples a different sequence of annual returns, so the median / 10th / 90th percentile lines are the range of outcomes, not guaranteed outcomes.
  • Lognormal returns. Growth each year is exp(log-return) rather than 1+return, so balances can never go below zero and the compounding captures volatility drag correctly.
  • Fat tails on stocks. Stock shocks are sampled from Student-t with 6 degrees of freedom, so large crashes show up as often as they do historically. Bonds and cash use normal samples.
  • Stock/bond correlation 0.10, cash independent. Correlation is constant (real-world correlations shift - 2022 was a notable example).
  • Withdrawal order in retirement: taxable, then tax-deferred (grossed up for taxes), then tax-free.
  • Contribution routing: 70% of your savings into tax-deferred, 30% into taxable. Employer match all goes tax-deferred.
  • Salary is flat within a career stage. Add another stage to model a raise or role change.
  • Same allocation across all account types. Linear glide path from current to retirement allocation.

Basic mode

  • Single effective tax rate applied to tax-deferred withdrawals.
  • Social Security = the flat annual benefit you enter, starting at claim age.
  • Return assumptions: stocks 6.8% real, bonds 2.0% real, cash 0.3% real (20th-century US historical averages).

Advanced mode adds

  • Return outlook picker: Optimistic, Historical, Forward-looking, or Cautious. Shifts the mean return of each asset class; the full Monte Carlo still runs.
  • 2026 federal tax brackets plus standard deduction and filing status (single / MFJ). Replaces the flat rate. Ordinary-income brackets only - no separate LTCG / qualified-dividends treatment, no NIIT, no IRMAA.
  • State tax as a flat rate on ordinary income.
  • Portfolio expense ratio (fee drag) subtracted from each year's return.
  • Social Security claim-age adjustment: your PIA times a multiplier (62 = 70%, 67 = 100% FRA, 70 = 124%).
  • RMDs from age 73 using the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table. Forced withdrawal from tax-deferred accounts even if you don't need the cash.

Not modeled (yet)

  • Healthcare and long-term care. Probably the largest missing risk. Pre-Medicare premiums, LTC events, and IRMAA surcharges are all absent.
  • Variable retirement spending. Research (Blanchett) shows spending follows a "smile" - higher in the go-go 60s, lower in 70s, higher again with late-life healthcare. This tool uses flat real spending.
  • Roth conversion optimizer. The tax valley between retirement and RMDs/SS is where big conversion savings live; not modeled.
  • Asset location. In reality, putting bonds in tax-deferred and growth in Roth adds 20-50 bps/year. This tool uses the same allocation across all account types.
  • Couples modeling. One person's ages, income, and Social Security only. No joint-life longevity, survivor benefits, or widow's-penalty tax bracket.
  • Longevity distribution. "Plan through age" is a fixed number. Real longevity has meaningful variance.
  • Historical sequence stress tests. Samples are parametric, not bootstrapped from actual bad sequences like 1966, 2000, or 1929.
  • Inflation variability. Inflation is assumed stable enough that working in real dollars is exact. Different expense categories (healthcare, education) inflate faster than CPI.

This tool is for educational planning only and is not financial advice. Talk to a licensed advisor before making major financial decisions.
